### Acute Left Heart Failure with Pulmonary Edema Clinical Intervention Guideline
**1. Trigger Conditions (Vital Sign Thresholds)**:
- Sudden severe dyspnea with oxygen saturation <90% and respiratory rate commonly >30 breaths/min.
- Frequently accompanied by hypertension or major blood pressure fluctuation, heart rate >110 beats/min, bilateral crackles, orthopnea, and pink frothy sputum.
- Systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg, worsening mental status, or progressive hypoxemia suggests impending respiratory or circulatory collapse.

**2. Immediate Interventions**:
- Place the patient upright or semi-recumbent, begin continuous ECG, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation monitoring, and provide oxygen immediately; if hypoxemia or work of breathing is significant, prioritize noninvasive ventilation such as CPAP or BiPAP.
- Give an IV loop diuretic such as furosemide and monitor urine output, renal function, and blood pressure response closely.
- If blood pressure allows, give IV nitrates to reduce preload and afterload; obtain bedside ultrasound, ECG, blood gas, and relevant laboratory studies to identify triggers such as ACS, tachyarrhythmia, or hypertensive crisis.
- If noninvasive ventilation fails, mental status declines, or fatigue worsens, prepare for endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation.

**3. Contraindications**:
- Avoid nitrates in patients with systolic blood pressure <100 mmHg, right ventricular infarction, or recent PDE-5 inhibitor use.
- Morphine should not be used routinely as standard therapy for pulmonary edema because it increases the risk of respiratory depression and hypotension.
- Avoid rapid large-volume fluid administration unless there is clear evidence of true hypovolemia.
- Patients with severe aortic stenosis require special caution with vasodilators and early cardiology involvement.

**4. Escalation**:
- Notify the on-call ICU resident immediately.
- If noninvasive ventilation is required, blood pressure becomes unstable, ACS is suspected, or intubation is being considered, notify the ICU attending and cardiology immediately; if circulatory collapse develops, escalate to the highest emergency response level.
