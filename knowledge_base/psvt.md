### Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT) Clinical Intervention Guideline
**1. Trigger Conditions (Vital Sign Thresholds)**:
- Sudden-onset, regular, usually narrow-complex tachycardia with a heart rate commonly between 150 and 250 beats/min.
- Hemodynamic instability marked by systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg, chest pain, acute heart failure, altered mental status, presyncope, or shock signs.
- Falling oxygen saturation, worsening dyspnea, or poor perfusion indicates the need for immediate escalation.

**2. Immediate Interventions**:
- Begin continuous cardiac, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation monitoring immediately, establish IV access, and obtain a 12-lead ECG; give oxygen if hypoxemic.
- In a regular narrow-complex rhythm with relative stability, attempt vagal maneuvers first.
- If vagal maneuvers fail and the rhythm remains regular and narrow-complex, give adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push followed by a rapid flush; a 12 mg repeat dose may be given if needed.
- If the patient is unstable, perform synchronized cardioversion immediately; provide sedation if feasible, but do not delay cardioversion waiting for sedation.

**3. Contraindications**:
- In irregular wide-complex tachycardia or suspected pre-excited atrial fibrillation, avoid empiric adenosine, verapamil, or diltiazem because deterioration may occur.
- Do not use adenosine in second-degree type II or third-degree AV block without a functioning pacemaker.
- Use adenosine with great caution in active bronchospasm or severe asthma.
- If synchronized cardioversion fails, escalate the energy level and request advanced life support or cardiology support without delay.

**4. Escalation**:
- Notify the on-call ICU resident promptly for stable PSVT.
- Immediately notify the ICU attending and cardiology/electrophysiology team for any hemodynamic instability, need for cardioversion, or uncertainty about rhythm diagnosis.
