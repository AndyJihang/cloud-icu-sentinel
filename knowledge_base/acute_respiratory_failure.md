# Acute Respiratory Failure

## Clinical Summary
Acute respiratory failure should be suspected when oxygenation declines, respiratory work increases, or mental status worsens in the setting of cardiopulmonary instability. Initial bedside management must prioritize airway assessment, verification of monitor accuracy, and rapid escalation when hypoxemia persists.

## Immediate Assessment
1. Confirm pulse oximetry waveform quality and sensor placement.
2. Assess airway patency, respiratory rate, work of breathing, and mental status.
3. Examine for cyanosis, accessory muscle use, agitation, and declining level of consciousness.
4. Review recent ventilator settings, oxygen delivery devices, and secretion burden when applicable.

## Initial Interventions
1. Apply or increase supplemental oxygen per ICU protocol.
2. Reposition the patient to optimize ventilation when clinically appropriate.
3. Suction secretions if obstruction is suspected.
4. Obtain arterial blood gas analysis if hypoxemia is persistent or unexplained.
5. Escalate to the responsible clinician or rapid response pathway if oxygen saturation remains below target despite intervention.

## Escalation Triggers
- Sustained SpO2 below 90 percent despite supplemental oxygen.
- Rapid deterioration in respiratory effort or mental status.
- Evidence of impending airway compromise.
- New hemodynamic instability accompanying respiratory decline.

## Documentation Guidance
Document the time of desaturation, assessed contributing factors, interventions performed, patient response, and the clinician notified.
